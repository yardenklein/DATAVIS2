A study on Australian and World Peace, 2009-2022

A visualisation created by Jordan Klein

In 2009, Australia ranked as the 10th most peaceful country in the world according to the Global Peace Index (GPI), which measures peace through various factors, including levels of violence, conflict, and militarization. This visualization explores the contributing factors behind Australia's peaceful environment and how they compare globally.

We start by analyzing Australia's domestic expenditure across key categories like healthcare, education, welfare, and military. This provides insight into how government spending might have influenced peace within the country. Afterward, we expand our view to compare peace levels and military expenditure globally, exploring the broader relationship between peace and defense spending.

Analysing Australia

Australian Expenditure by State, 2009

This section delves into Australia's domestic expenditure for 2009 and how it contributed to the country's peaceful environment. By examining government spending on social welfare, education, healthcare, and environmental sustainability, we can understand the key factors that contributed to Australia's peace and stability in 2009.

We focus on 2009 because military expenditure was the highest category of government spending that year, which coincided with Australia's 10th place ranking in the Global Peace Index. This provides a plausible reason to explore the relationship between military spending and peace, both within Australia and globally.

Military Expenditure Comparison

GPI Vs Military Expenditure, 2009-2022

Military expenditure is a crucial factor in understanding both security and peace. While Australia spends significantly less on defense compared to global powers like the United States, China, and Russia, it still plays an important role in global peace. Factors beyond military spending contribute to peace, and this comparison of Australiaâ€™s budget with top global military spenders helps assess these dynamics, highlighting how countries like Australia can maintain high peace rankings despite lower military investments.

The scatter plot above shows the Average Military Expenditure vs the Average GPI from 2009 to 2022. Despite Australia's high expenditure relative to many other nations in the region, it maintains a higher GPI compared to countries like Russia and China, who pair high military spending with lower peace rankings. This comparison highlights the complex relationship between peace and military investment, where higher spending does not always translate to higher peace rankings.

Average Military Expenditure by Continent, 2009-2022

The bar chart illustrates the average military expenditure by continent from 2009 to 2022. As shown, North America has the highest military expenditure by a significant margin, reflecting its dominant global military presence, while Oceania and South America have the lowest. This comparison highlights how different regions prioritize defense spending, with Asia and Europe occupying middle ground in terms of their defense budgets.

Military spending is often linked to broader geopolitical strategies. For example, countries in North America and Europe maintain higher defense budgets to support both internal and external security strategies. However, as shown in Australia's case, higher military spending does not necessarily equate to higher peace rankings. This analysis suggests that while military investment is critical for defense, factors like diplomacy and governance also play a crucial role in a nation's peacefulness.

The line graph presents trends in the Average GPI by continent from 2009 to 2022. Although North America demonstrates higher military spending, its peace ranking remains comparable to other continents. This suggests that global peace is shaped by multiple factors, such as social policies, governance, and economic conditions, rather than military expenditure alone. Notably, regions like Oceania and Europe show stability in their GPI rankings despite lower defense budgets, indicating that countries with lower military spending may focus on other areas to foster peace.
